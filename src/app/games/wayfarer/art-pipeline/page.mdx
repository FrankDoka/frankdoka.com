## The goal

Every character in [Wayfarer](/games/wayfarer) — heroes and enemies — is generated **locally**, on my own RTX 4090,
from a text prompt. No paid services, no stock art. The pipeline has to turn a prompt like *"sexy valkyrie warrior,
silver armor, glowing spear"* into a **game-ready sprite**: a cute, on-style pixel-art character on a **clean
transparent background** that looks right on any background the game throws at it.

That last part — *clean transparent edges* — turned out to be the hard problem, and it took several sessions to solve
properly. This page documents the whole pipeline, including that detour, because the lesson was worth keeping.

## The stack

| Piece | Tool |
|---|---|
| Image model | **Illustrious-XL** (SDXL) via **ComfyUI**, driven over its HTTP API |
| Style | a pixel-art **"birdman" LoRA** (clip-skip −2) for the consistent gacha look |
| Background detection | **BiRefNet** (semantic — understands *"this shape is a character"*) |
| Animation | **Wan 2.1 image-to-video** + a matching pixel-art animation LoRA |
| Asset processing | **Node.js + sharp** (the matting, trimming, packing) |

## Generating a character

A single script (`forge.mjs`) does everything for a static character, talking to ComfyUI's API:

1. **Generate** the character on a white background (Illustrious + the pixel LoRA, 30 steps).
2. **Export two things**: the raw RGB image *and* a soft BiRefNet mask of where the character is.
3. **Matte** the two together (see below) into a clean transparent PNG.
4. **Trim** to the character and resize onto a 512×512 canvas.

The prompts lean into a cute, sexy, gacha-splash style, with heavy negative prompts (`deformed, malformed, blob,
fused, extra limbs…`) to suppress the rough anatomy AI loves to produce. One command re-rolls any character on a new
seed until it lands.

## The transparent-edge problem (and the wrong turns)

Here's the thing that ate several sessions. When you cut a character out of its background, the pixels *right on the
edge* are a **blend** of the character and the background. Generate on white, and every silhouette edge picks up a
little white — so the cutout has a faint **white halo** that screams against the game's dark background.

I tried, in order:

- **Hard mask + erosion** — ate the silhouette and still leaked.
- **"Remove pixels brighter than the core"** — couldn't tell a white *halo* from a white *outfit*. Fundamental
  ambiguity: a high-opacity white edge pixel is indistinguishable from real white art.
- **A dark outline over the edge** — bulletproof on a dark background, but it only covered the *outer* silhouette
  (not the white trapped *inside* hair or on a thin blade), and it traced noise into black speckle on animation frames.
- **A green screen** — the model **tinted the character** green (green hair). AI green-screens contaminate the subject.
- **A dedicated pixel-art background-remover node** — its detection keys by *colour*, and our characters span the full
  range (light skin to dark capes), so it couldn't separate them from the light-grey backdrop at all.

Every one of these was fighting the symptom.

## The actual fix: alpha matting

The right answer is a standard technique I should have reached for first — **alpha matting with colour
decontamination**. Instead of *keeping* the background-blended edge, you **recover the true edge colour** by
subtracting the background the edge absorbed:

```
true_color = (pixel − (1 − alpha) · background) / alpha
```

With the soft BiRefNet mask as the alpha and the background colour sampled from the image corners, this gives every
edge pixel its *real* colour back. A white-blended edge on a red cloak becomes red; a clean edge that's genuinely
white stays white. The result is a cutout that looks clean on **any** background — verified against the harshest test,
bright magenta, where any leftover white fringe would be obvious. No outline, no heuristics, no whack-a-mole.

The whole messy post-processing pipeline got deleted and replaced with this one principled step.

## Animation

Characters can also be animated with **Wan 2.1 image-to-video**: feed it a sprite plus a motion prompt
(`p1x3lStyl3, standing idle, breathing`), and it generates a short clip in the matching pixel-art style. A second
pipeline removes the per-frame backgrounds, packs the frames into a spritesheet (with global alignment so the
character doesn't jitter), and the game plays it as a looping idle. A `sharp` + `ffmpeg` step also exports a
shareable transparent **animated WebP** for showing off.

## What I'd tell past me

Two lessons. First: **when an edge looks clean at low zoom on a dark background, it isn't — zoom to 8× and check on
magenta.** Second, and bigger: **reach for the standard, principled technique before inventing heuristics.** Alpha
matting is a solved problem; I spent days rebuilding a worse version of it by hand before doing it right.
