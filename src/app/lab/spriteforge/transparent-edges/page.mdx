Part of the **[SpriteForge](/lab/spriteforge)** dev log.

## The problem

A game sprite needs a **clean transparent background** that looks right on anything the game draws behind
it. But the characters are generated on a solid background, and the pixels *right on the edge* are a
**blend** of the character and that background. Generate on white, and every silhouette edge picks up a
little white — a faint **halo** that screams against a dark game background.

## The wrong turns

I tried, in order, and each one leaked:

- **Hard mask + erosion** — ate into the silhouette and still left fringe.
- **"Remove pixels brighter than the core"** — couldn't tell a white *halo* from a white *outfit*. A
  high-opacity white edge pixel is fundamentally indistinguishable from real white art.
- **A dark outline over the edge** — bulletproof on a dark background, but it only covered the *outer*
  silhouette (not white trapped inside hair or on a thin blade), and it traced noise into black speckle
  on animation frames.
- **A green screen** — the model **tinted the character** green. AI green-screens contaminate the subject.
- **A dedicated background-remover node** — keys by *colour*, and our characters span the full range, so
  it couldn't separate them from the backdrop at all.

Every one of these was fighting the symptom.

## The actual fix: alpha matting

The right answer is a standard technique I should have reached for first — **alpha matting with colour
decontamination**. Instead of *keeping* the background-blended edge, you **recover the true edge colour**
by subtracting the background the edge absorbed:

```
true_color = (pixel − (1 − alpha) · background) / alpha
```

With a soft **BiRefNet** mask as the alpha and the background colour sampled from the image corners, every
edge pixel gets its real colour back. A white-blended edge on a red cloak becomes red; an edge that's
genuinely white stays white. The result is clean on **any** background — verified against the harshest
test, bright magenta, where any leftover fringe would be obvious:

![The same sprite, clean on white, dark, and magenta](./cover.png)

That's the hardest case — silver hair, gold armour, white stockings, all light-on-light — and there's no
halo anywhere. No outline, no heuristics, no whack-a-mole. The whole messy post-processing pipeline got
deleted and replaced with this one principled step (`lib/matte.mjs`).

## What I'd tell past me

**Reach for the standard, principled technique before inventing heuristics.** Alpha matting is a solved
problem; I spent days rebuilding a worse version of it by hand before doing it right. And: **when an edge
looks clean at low zoom on a dark background, it isn't — zoom to 8× and check on magenta.**
